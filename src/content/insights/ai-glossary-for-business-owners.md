## The 10 AI terms for business owners that actually matter

### 1. Large language model (LLM)

The engine behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and a growing list of software your team already uses. LLMs learned to predict what comes next in text — which makes them useful for drafting, summarising, classifying, and answering questions. You don't need to understand how they're built. What matters: you're almost certainly already using one, whether it's in your Xero assistant, your Intercom chatbot, or the "generate a summary" button in your CRM.

### 2. Prompt

What you type into an AI tool. The quality of your prompt determines the quality of the output. "Write a follow-up email" gets you something generic. "Write a two-paragraph follow-up to a warm property lead who attended Saturday's open home — casual tone, mention the council approval" gets you something worth sending.

Getting better at prompting is the cheapest skill improvement available to your team. It costs nothing and the return is immediate.

### 3. Automation vs AI

People use these interchangeably. They're different. **Automation** runs a fixed rule: invoice arrives → code it to account 1234. **AI** handles judgment calls: "which of these 300 leads should I call first?" or "is this support ticket urgent?" Most of what SMBs call "AI" is automation with AI handling the exceptions. That distinction matters because automation is predictable; AI sometimes surprises you.

### 4. API

An Application Programming Interface is how two software systems talk to each other without anyone copying and pasting. When a vendor says "we'll connect your CRM to AI via the API," it means: your software sends data to the AI, gets a result back, and acts on it automatically. You don't write the API — your developer or implementation partner does. What it means for you: AI becomes part of your workflow instead of a separate tab you have to remember to open.

### 5. RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)

When an AI tool uses *your* documents — policies, product guides, past emails, pricing sheets — to answer questions rather than relying on its general training, that's RAG. It's why a well-built support chatbot answers accurately about your specific products, while a generic chatbot invents return policies you don't have. For most SMBs, getting RAG right is the entire game. A chatbot trained on your own knowledge base is dramatically more useful than a general-purpose one.

### 6. Hallucination

AI tools generate plausible-sounding text that's factually wrong. A legal AI might cite a court case that doesn't exist. A support bot might quote a price you discontinued last year. This is called hallucination, and it's not a bug that will be patched — it's an inherent behaviour. The answer isn't to avoid AI; it's to keep a human reviewing any output that touches money, legal, medical, or public communications. Not every output, every time — just the ones where a mistake has real consequences.

### 7. AI agent

A standard AI tool answers questions. An agent takes actions — books a calendar slot, fires an email, updates a CRM record, searches the web, or triggers a workflow. Agents are the fastest-growing area in AI right now and the most error-prone. An agent that can book meetings can also book the wrong ones.

### 8. Fine-tuning

Training an AI model on your specific data so it behaves in a particular way. You hear this pitched often. Most SMBs don't need it. Off-the-shelf models with good prompts and your documents via RAG handle roughly 90% of business use cases without fine-tuning. Fine-tuning makes sense for high-volume, highly specific tasks where you have thousands of labelled examples. If a vendor pitches fine-tuning as your starting point, ask why RAG isn't sufficient first.

### 9. Context window

The amount of text an AI can "hold in mind" during a session. A short context window means the model forgets earlier parts of a long conversation. This matters when you're asking AI to analyse a 50-page contract or a large data export. Modern models handle substantially more than they did two years ago, but it's worth checking if your use case involves very long documents.

### 10. Chatbot vs assistant vs AI workflow

- **Chatbot**: answers questions in a chat interface, limited to what it knows or has been given access to
- **AI assistant**: more capable; drafts, summarises, integrates with tools, maintains context across a longer session
- **AI workflow**: a chain of AI steps that runs automatically — data in, action out — with no human step in the middle

Most SMBs start with an assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Copilot) and graduate to workflows once they trust the outputs enough to remove the manual review step.
