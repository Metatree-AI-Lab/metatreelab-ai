Independent travel agents face a real squeeze right now. Booking.com and Expedia commoditised the transactional side of travel. What the platforms can't do — curated, complex itineraries, supplier relationships, knowing that the beachfront room in Santorini actually floods in November — is still where agents earn their keep. But back-office time kills that advantage. If you're spending 3 hours building a draft itinerary before you even know whether the client will book, you're giving away the margin that justifies your existence.

AI doesn't fix your business model. It does fix that time problem. And in a 2-to-5-person agency, fixing that one thing changes a lot.

## The itinerary problem

A detailed multi-destination itinerary — say, 10 days across Japan or a 14-night Europe circuit — typically takes 2.5 to 4 hours to build from scratch: researching routes, writing day-by-day notes, checking transfer times, sourcing hotels and experiences at different price points, formatting it all into something a client will actually read.

AI drafts the skeleton in 15 to 20 minutes.

Feed it the client brief — budget, dates, travel style, past trips, the non-negotiables — and a good AI assistant (Claude or ChatGPT work well here; Travefy has AI assist built in) will produce a structured draft with day headings, suggested activities, accommodation tiers, and logical routing. It won't know your preferred supplier for Kyoto ryokans, and it'll occasionally suggest a connection that doesn't exist. Your job is to fix those things in 45 minutes instead of building from zero.

For a 2-person agency quoting 6-8 itineraries a week, that's 15 to 20 hours saved. Per week. That's either more clients, or you going home on time.

## Out-of-hours inquiries are where you're leaking leads

Here's a number that surprises most agents: 40-50% of initial travel inquiries land outside business hours. People research holidays at night, on weekends, or during their lunch break. If you reply the next business morning, you're competing with whoever replied at 10pm.

A simple AI assistant — trained on your agency's destinations, price ranges, travel styles, and process — can handle that first conversation. It qualifies the lead, captures the brief, and either books a consultation or gives the client enough detail to feel confident they're dealing with an expert. You wake up to a warm lead with a completed brief rather than a cold "can you help with Bali in October?"

One boutique Sydney agency added a chat assistant to their website in early 2026. Within three months, after-hours lead capture went from near-zero to accounting for 35% of new bookings. Their close rate on those leads was slightly higher than phone inquiries — partly because clients who self-qualify via a detailed chat are more serious.

This isn't magic. The assistant needs good training material: your FAQs, your destination strengths, how you price, what you need from a client before you can quote. Spend a day on that setup and it runs indefinitely.

## What AI can't do (be honest about this)

AI will not replace your supplier relationships. It doesn't know that the Maldives overwater bungalow it suggested is currently being renovated, that the Amalfi transfer company you've used for 10 years is dramatically more reliable than the one with better SEO, or that a particular family needs a ground-floor room because the grandmother has a hip problem.

It also makes mistakes on logistics — particularly inter-country connections, visa requirements, and anything involving current pricing. Treat every AI draft as a starting point, not a finished product. Any itinerary that goes to a client should have a human check on routing and pricing.

The agents who get burned are the ones who publish AI outputs without review. The ones who succeed use it as a fast, smart first draft and add the expertise that actually earned the client's trust.

## Competing with the platforms

The real competitive question isn't "can a travel agent use AI." It's "who uses it better." Booking.com and Expedia have engineering teams you can't match. They don't have your personal knowledge of a 90-room family resort in Tuscany that doesn't appear on any aggregator, or your 15-year relationship with the ground operator in Vietnam.

AI narrows the admin gap so you can spend more time on the thing the platforms genuinely cannot replicate: expert, human curation. That's the business.
