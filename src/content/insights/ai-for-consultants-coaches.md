Most consultants and coaches hit the same wall. They're good at what they do, clients pay well for it — and then they run out of hours. The business is capped at 25-30 billable hours a week, and every hour not spent delivering is spent writing proposals, onboarding clients, chasing invoices, or trying to keep a LinkedIn presence alive.

For a typical solo practitioner, that non-billable overhead easily eats 8 or more hours a week. None of it is billable. All of it has to happen anyway.

AI doesn't change what you know. What it changes is how much of your week goes toward proving it and packaging it.

## The two leverage points for consultants and coaches

Your situation is different from a retailer or a restaurant. You don't have inventory to manage or shift schedules to fill. Your two biggest time drains are probably:

**Non-billable practice overhead** — proposals, reports, onboarding documents, follow-up emails, LinkedIn posts, and the hourly admin that glues all of it together. Most consultants spend 30–40% of their week here and bill none of it.

**The 1:1 ceiling** — every insight you deliver is delivered once, to one client. If you could package even a portion of that expertise into something someone pays for without needing you in the room, your revenue cap disappears.

AI, used well, cuts into both.

## Cutting proposal and reporting time by 80%

The proposal is the archetype of high-stakes, time-consuming work that is 70% template. You're describing the problem you'll solve, your approach, your credentials, your price. The structure barely changes; the specifics do.

A Claude or GPT-4o prompt fed with your past proposals, a short brief about the new client, and your standard methodology produces a solid draft in 10–15 minutes. What used to take three hours becomes a 30-minute editing job — you're checking facts, sharpening the hook, and adding the specific detail that shows you actually listened in the discovery call.

The same applies to end-of-engagement reports, progress summaries, and workshop runbooks. Feed the AI your notes and raw outputs; it returns a structured draft. You spend your time on substance, not formatting.

## From 1:1 time to packaged products

Here's the more interesting play: taking the knowledge you deliver in $400–600/hour sessions and turning it into something a client can buy for $497 without needing you present.

Coaches have sold courses for 20 years. What's new is how fast you can build them. AI can turn a transcript of your best workshop into a structured course outline, write the lesson scripts, generate quiz questions, draft the sales page, and produce the email sequence — in days, not months.

A leadership coach I worked with had been running the same two-day team communication workshop for eight years. She knew the content cold; she'd just never had time to package it. We used AI to turn her workshop transcript, her slide notes, and her client Q&A archive into a structured self-paced online program. She launched it at $997. In the first year it generated $24k in revenue she didn't need to show up for.

That's not life-changing on its own — but it was incremental to her existing 1:1 practice, and it took about 30 hours of her time to build. The math on productising your IP gets interesting quickly.

## What AI cannot do here

AI cannot replicate the trust you've built over years with clients who bring you their hardest problems. It cannot do the room-reading that makes a good facilitator irreplaceable. It cannot generate your proprietary frameworks — it can only help you document and scale them once you've done the thinking.

The consultants misusing AI right now are the ones outsourcing the intellectual work. That's where credibility evaporates fast, and clients notice. The right use is the opposite: let AI handle the packaging, the formatting, and the writing — keep the hard thinking yours.

## The practical next step

If you have 5+ years of IP in your head and you're still delivering all of it 1:1, the fastest path to a different model is to start transcribing. Record a session, a workshop, a client call (with permission). Feed it to Claude and ask for a structured outline. You'll see immediately whether there's a product in there.

The technical lift is smaller than most consultants expect. What holds people back isn't capability — it's the belief that packaging takes months. With the right tools, a first version takes weeks.
