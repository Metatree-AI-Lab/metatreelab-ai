The pipeline problem in event planning is almost always the same. An inquiry comes in on a Tuesday at 10pm. Your coordinator sees it Thursday morning, fires off a templated reply, and by the time a proper proposal lands in the client's inbox it's Friday. They've booked someone else. Meanwhile your best people are spending their afternoons rebuilding run sheets from last month's files and chasing vendor confirmations that could have gone out automatically.

AI doesn't fix the creative or relational side of events — that's still yours. What it does is strip out the three or four hours of admin that surround every booking, so the same team can handle significantly more volume.

## Where event planners actually lose time

Before buying any tools, map where the hours go. For most small events businesses doing 8-30 events a month, the week looks roughly like this:

- Drafting replies to initial inquiries: 4+ hours, often fragmented across the day
- Writing or customising proposals and quotes: 2.5 hours per new client
- Coordinating vendors via email: 2 hours of back-and-forth per event
- Building run sheets from scratch: 1.5 hours per event
- Post-event follow-ups and testimonial requests: 1-1.5 hours per event

That's 12+ admin hours per event — work that is largely templatable.

## Inquiry response: the fastest win

When someone fills in your contact form at 9pm, an AI assistant trained on your packages, pricing, and FAQs can reply within two minutes, confirm availability, and send a link to book a discovery call. No human involvement until the call itself.

Tools like a custom assistant on the Anthropic or OpenAI API, wired to your CRM via Zapier, handle this well. You feed it your brochure, your event packages, your FAQ doc, and a few example replies you like the tone of. The result doesn't sound robotic — it sounds like someone on your team who is actually awake at 9pm.

A Sydney-based events company with six staff was averaging 4+ hours a week on initial inquiry responses alone. After setting up an AI intake assistant, that dropped to under 30 minutes of review and approval time. Their median inquiry-to-proposal gap fell from 28 hours to under 3 hours. Across all admin tasks, they recovered roughly 9 hours a week — at their coordinator rates of around $55 per hour loaded, that's approximately $26,000 in recovered staff time annually.

## Proposal generation: stop starting from scratch

A well-structured event proposal is mostly repeatable — venue options, package inclusions, pricing tiers, your credentials, and a few personalised lines about the client's brief. Feed your best proposals into a template and let an AI assistant generate a first draft from the discovery call notes.

Tools like Notion AI or a Claude-powered script can produce a solid 80% proposal in under five minutes. Your coordinator fills in the specific touches, checks the pricing, and sends. What used to take 2.5 hours now takes around 40 minutes.

At scale, that's not a trivial number. If you're doing 20 proposals a month, you're recovering roughly 40 hours — the equivalent of a full week of coordinator time, every month.

## Vendor coordination and run sheets

The coordination chain for a mid-size event (caterer, AV, florist, venue, photographer) can generate 50-80 emails. AI drafting helps with individual messages, but the bigger leverage is templated run sheets that update automatically as details change, with confirmation emails triggered to vendors at set milestones.

A simple Zapier + AI workflow can send a briefing email to each vendor 7 days out, a confirmation 48 hours before, and a day-of contact sheet on the morning — all from a single source-of-truth spreadsheet or CRM record. The coordinator doesn't need to touch it unless a vendor replies with a problem.

## What AI doesn't replace

Be clear-eyed about the limits. AI is bad at anything that requires real judgment under pressure — a venue change at 6pm the night before, a difficult client on the day, reading a room and adapting the schedule in real time. Those are the moments your clients are actually paying for.

The trap to avoid: automating so aggressively that you strip out the human touches that make clients come back. A personalised thank-you note, a post-event call, remembering that the client's CEO prefers no speeches — none of that should be handed to a bot.
